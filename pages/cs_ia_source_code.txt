<div class="modal fade" id="pp-modal" tabindex="-1" role="document">

            	<div class="modal-content"> 
                	<div class="close-modal" data-dismiss="modal">
                    	<div class="modal-header">
                    		<h4 class="modal-title" id = "Position-Paper"> Guide to Position Paper Writing</h4>
                    	</div>
                    	<div class = "modal-body">
A position paper is essentially an essay which delineates your country’s/organization’s stand on the agenda. It helps you define the sequence for any ideas you may present in committee. This isn’t too difficult a task if you have researched your position well.
<br>
The chairs often ask for them a week prior to the conference in order to obtain an idea of the direction in which the discussion is headed. They also factor it in when making decisions about awarding best delegates. Your position paper should not only highlight your country’s policies but also suggest resolutions. A good starting off point is often the study guide that is provided by your selected committee.
<br>
Introduce your country and it’s historic relationship with the agenda and the committee.
Explain how the issue effects your country.
What policies pertain to this issue in your country and the justification for the same.
Facts and figures to substantiate your claims.
Previous UN conventions and resolutions signed or ratified by your country in relation to this issue.
Other UN policies supported by your country.
Recommendations for resolutions.
How does the position of other countries affect your country.

                    		<h4 class="modal-title" id = "reso-modal"> Writing a resolution</h4>
                    	</div>
                    	<div class = "modal-body">
                    		
<p>
A resolution is a document that contains all the issues the committee tried to solve and proposed solutions. A resolution which hasn’t been voted upon is called a draft resolution. It only turns into a resolution once it is passed during voting bloc.
</p>
<p>
Any delegation can write a resolution. The lead author or the one who initiates the resolution writing is called a sponsor. Multiple delegations may sponsor a resolution if significant contributions have come in.
</p>
<p>
A resolution is typically written during unmoderated caucus. During this time you can collaborate with other delegates and try and incorporate the view of as many delegations as possible. Work on resolution usually begins after multiple debates have been held.
</p>
<p>
The resolution can be looked upon as the final aim of a committee. All the speeches, lobbying and teamwork should build up to this. The strength of a committee is often reflected by their ability to pass a resolution and it’s quality.
</p>
<p>
A resolution must be formatted in the following manner:
</p>
<u>Heading:</u>
<p>
    4 pieces of information:
<ol>
 <li>Committee Name </li>
 <li>Sponsors </li>
 <li>Signatories(delegations that don’t contribute yet
want to see the resolution debated) </li> 
<li>The topic</li>
</ol>
<p>
<u>Pre-ambulatory clauses:</u>

Contains all the issues the committee wants to resolve. Contains reasons why the committee is working on this issue and information on previous courses of action undertaken by the committee. A general layout would be:

<u>Underlined pre-ambulatory phrase</u> + statement + comma
</p>
<p>
<u>Operative clauses:</u>

Operative clauses states the solutions you have arrived at. It should address the issues specifically mentioned in the pre-ambulatory clauses above it.

A general format is:

Serial Number + Operative Clause phrase + solution + semi-colon(period in case of last clause)

You may have sub operative clauses to cover anything you think may not be adequately covered in your main clause.
</p>
<p>
<u>Amendments:</u>
<br>
Amendments are of two types:
<ol>
<li> Friendly: A change to the draft resolution that all sponsors agree with. It must be signed by all sponsors and approved by the committee chair.</li>
<li> Unfriendly amendment: A change not supported by all sponsors and requires voting. A certain number of signatories will be required to introduce it. Prior to voting on the draft resolution, the committee must vote on unfriendly amendments.</li>
</ol>
</p>
